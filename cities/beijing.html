Beijing

Ancient Capital & Modern Metropolis

Detailed Overview

Beijing, the capital of the People's Republic of China, stands as one of the world's great ancient capitals with over 3,000 years of history and 860 years as a capital city. As China's political, cultural, and educational center, Beijing represents both the glorious imperial past and the dynamic modern present of this rising global power. The city's layout follows strict feng shui principles, with the Forbidden City at its heart, surrounded by concentric rings of development that tell the story of China's evolution from imperial dynasty to modern superpower. Beijing's historical significance is unparalleled in China. It served as the capital during the Liao, Jin, Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties, leaving behind an incredible architectural legacy. The Forbidden City, the world's largest palace complex, contains 980 buildings with 8,886 rooms, housing the Imperial Palace Museum's collection of over 1.8 million artifacts. Just north of the palace lies Jingshan Park, where the last Ming emperor hanged himself as rebel forces entered the city in 1644. The Temple of Heaven, where emperors performed annual ceremonies to ensure good harvests, showcases exquisite Ming dynasty architecture and remains one of Beijing's most photographed sites. The Great Wall of China, which snakes through Beijing's northern districts, represents one of humanity's most ambitious engineering projects. While sections like Badaling attract massive crowds, more remote segments like Jiankou and Jinshanling offer breathtaking hikes along crumbling watchtowers with minimal tourist interference. Modern Beijing contrasts sharply with its ancient heritage. The Central Business District (CBD) boasts some of Asia's most striking contemporary architecture, including the CCTV Headquarters (known locally as "Big Pants") and the China Zun Tower, currently Beijing's tallest building at 528 meters. Beijing's cultural scene is equally impressive. The 798 Art District, housed in a former electronics factory complex, has become one of Asia's most important contemporary art centers. Traditional performances like Peking Opera at the Liyuan Theatre and acrobatic shows at the Chaoyang Theatre showcase China's artistic heritage. The city's culinary landscape ranges from imperial banquet cuisine at Fangshan Restaurant in Beihai Park to humble street food in the hutongs (ancient alleyways). Beijing's famous Peking Duck, with its crispy skin and tender meat wrapped in thin pancakes with scallions and sweet bean sauce, remains a must-try culinary experience. The city faces significant challenges, particularly air pollution that occasionally blankets the city in thick smog. However, massive government investment in green technology and public transportation has led to noticeable improvements in recent years. Beijing's metro system, now the world's largest by route length, continues to expand, making the city increasingly navigable for visitors. Despite its rapid modernization, Beijing retains its historical soul in places like the Shichahai lakes district, where locals still practice tai chi at dawn, and the Lama Temple, where Tibetan Buddhism continues to thrive in the heart of Han Chinese culture.

Overview

Beijing, China's capital for over 800 years, is a city where ancient history meets modern innovation. From the majestic Forbidden City to the contemporary architecture of the CBD, Beijing offers a journey through China's imperial past and dynamic present.

Travel Tips

Comprehensive Travel Guide

Navigating Beijing requires strategic planning due to its vast size and occasional air quality issues. For first-time visitors, purchasing a transportation card (Yikatong) is essential for seamless travel on the extensive metro system, which now spans over 700 kilometers across 27 lines. Download mobile apps like Baidu Maps (China's Google Maps equivalent) and Didi (China's Uber) for navigation and ride-hailing services, as Google services are restricted in China. Ticket purchasing for major attractions demands advance planning. The Forbidden City limits daily visitors to 80,000, with tickets often selling out days in advance during peak seasons. Book online through official channels at least a week before your visit. Similarly, the Great Wall sections closest to Beijing (Badaling and Mutianyu) can become uncomfortably crowded. Consider visiting Jinshanling or Simatai for a more authentic experience, though these require longer travel times. Hiring a private driver for Great Wall trips often proves more convenient than public transportation. Beijing's climate presents distinct challenges across seasons. Spring (April-May) brings pleasant temperatures but occasional sandstorms from the Gobi Desert. Summer (June-August) can be oppressively hot and humid, with temperatures exceeding 35°C (95°F). Autumn (September-October) offers ideal conditions with clear skies and comfortable temperatures. Winter (November-March) brings freezing temperatures but fewer tourists and the possibility of seeing the Great Wall dusted with snow. Cultural etiquette remains important. When visiting temples, dress modestly and walk clockwise around stupas and prayer wheels. Bargaining is expected at markets like the Silk Street and Panjiayuan Antique Market, but start at about 30% of the asking price. Tipping is not customary in China and can sometimes cause confusion. Instead, excellent service warrants sincere thanks and possibly returning to the same establishment. Health considerations include bringing N95 masks for days with poor air quality (check AQI levels via apps like AirVisual). Drink only bottled or boiled water, and be cautious with street food—stick to vendors with high turnover. Pharmacies (yaodian) are plentiful, but bring any prescription medications with you, as equivalents may not be available. Register with your embassy upon arrival and keep emergency contact numbers handy. Money matters require attention. While mobile payments (WeChat Pay and Alipay) dominate, international credit cards work at major hotels and upscale restaurants. Always carry some cash for smaller establishments and markets. Currency exchange is available at banks and hotels, but rates at the airport are less favorable. Notify your bank of travel plans to avoid card blocks on international transactions.
