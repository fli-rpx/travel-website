Nanjing

The Ancient Capital of Six Dynasties

Overview

Nanjing is the capital of Jiangsu Province and a historically significant city that served as the capital of several Chinese dynasties. Located on the Yangtze River, Nanjing is known for its rich history, cultural heritage, and beautiful natural scenery.

Must-See Highlights

Top Attractions

Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum, Confucius Temple, Nanjing City Wall, Xuanwu Lake

Local Cuisine

Nanjing Salted Duck, Duck Blood Soup, Tangbao Soup Dumplings

Best Time to Visit

Autumn (September-November) with pleasant weather and clear skies

Travel Tips

Comprehensive Travel Guide

Exploring Nanjing's historical sites requires sensitivity to the city's complex past and strategic planning for its extensive attractions. The Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall demands significant emotional preparation—allocate 2-3 hours for a thorough visit and consider visiting earlier in the day when you have more emotional energy. Photography is restricted in certain areas, and respectful silence is expected throughout. The memorial is closed on Mondays, and advance online reservation is recommended, especially during national holidays and anniversaries of the massacre (mid-December). Navigating Nanjing's dispersed historical sites is facilitated by its metro system, though some attractions require additional transportation. The Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum and Sun Yat-sen's Mausoleum are both located within Zhongshan Mountain National Park—consider purchasing the combined ticket that includes park shuttle bus access between sites. The preserved sections of the City Wall are accessible at multiple gates: the Zhonghua Gate (largest ancient city gate in China) offers the most impressive fortifications, while the Xuanwu Lake section provides pleasant lakeside walking. The Confucius Temple area is best visited in the late afternoon to enjoy both daytime sights and evening lantern displays along the Qinhuai River. Accommodation choices should balance accessibility to historical sites with modern comforts. The Xinjiekou area serves as Nanjing's modern commercial center with numerous international hotel chains and excellent metro connectivity but lacks historical character. The Confucius Temple area offers hotels with traditional architectural elements and immediate access to the Qinhuai River district but can be crowded and noisy, especially on weekends. The Gulou (Drum Tower) area provides a good compromise with proximity to Nanjing University, decent transportation links, and more local dining options. For visitors focusing on the massacre memorial and related sites, consider hotels near the Yunjinlu metro station. Nanjing's culinary scene reflects its Jiangsu province location with emphasis on freshwater ingredients, careful preparation, and balanced flavors. Must-try dishes include Nanjing salted duck (yanshui ya), duck blood and vermicelli soup (yaxue fensi tang), and lion's head meatballs (shizitou). The Confucius Temple food street offers concentrated culinary experiences but can be touristy—venture into side streets for more authentic options. For a unique dining experience, visit one of Nanjing's traditional duck restaurants that have specialized in the dish for generations. Local breakfast typically consists of sesame cakes (shaobing), fried dough sticks (youtiao), and soybean milk (doujiang)—available at neighborhood eateries throughout the city. Cultural experiences require understanding Nanjing's historical context. The Nanjing Museum's collections are exceptional but extensive—focus on highlights: the imperial porcelain collection, Jiangsu region archaeological finds, and Republican-era artifacts. The Presidential Palace offers insight into China's political transitions but can be confusing without context—consider renting an audio guide or joining an English-language tour if available. For performing arts, the Nanjing Baiju Opera House presents performances of local Kunqu opera, one of China's oldest operatic forms. Calligraphy enthusiasts should visit the Nanjing Calligraphy and Painting Academy or the stone carvings at Qixia Temple. Day trips from Nanjing reveal the surrounding Yangtze River Delta's historical and natural attractions. Yangzhou (1 hour by high-speed train), a former canal trading hub, offers the Slender West Lake gardens and historic streets. Zhenjiang (30 minutes by high-speed train), known for its vinegar production, features the Jinshan Temple and Beigu Mountain. The ancient village of Zhouzhuang (2 hours by combination of train and bus) preserves traditional water town architecture, though it can be crowded. For natural scenery, the Purple Mountain (within Nanjing) offers extensive hiking trails and additional historical sites beyond the major mausoleums. The Qixia Mountain and its Buddhist temple complex (40 minutes by taxi) provide autumn foliage viewing when the ginkgo trees turn golden in November.
