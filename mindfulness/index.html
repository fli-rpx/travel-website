Why Your Brain Loves Certain Colors and Looks

Your eyes use 44% of your brain's energy. Because seeing is expensive, your brain rewards "easy" visuals with dopamine and endorphins.

The Science: Your brain is an energy miser. When you see something visually "fluent" (symmetrical, clear, appealing), your brain goes "Ah, that was easy!" and gives you a pleasure reward.

‚ö†Ô∏è

The Catch: Just because something feels good to look at doesn't mean it's good for you. It can be empty calories for your eyes.

üß†

The Stroop Effect: Colors Can Hijack Your Brain

Visual information competes with conceptual information in your brain‚Äîand sometimes visuals win automatically.

The Experiment: Show someone the word "RED" written in blue ink. Their brain slows down because it has to fight between the word and the color.

üí°

What This Means: Colors and appearances have power over your attention whether you want them to or not. Your brain processes them automatically before you can decide.

‚ú®

Aesthetic Arrest: When Beauty Stops You Cold

That feeling when you're "stopped dead in your tracks" by something beautiful‚Äîa temporary escape from desire and struggle.

Philosopher Schopenhauer called this becoming a "pure, will-less, painless subject of knowledge." For a moment, you forget your problems and cravings.

‚ö†Ô∏è

The Problem: You're not in control during aesthetic arrest. The thing controls you. You're a spectator, not an agent.

üé≠

Animism: Why Objects Feel Alive

Humans naturally project life and intention onto inanimate things‚Äîjudging them by Evaluation, Potency, and Activity.

Your brain is wired to treat certain things as if they have power or intention. That's why a color can feel "strong" or a face can feel "magnetic."

üí°

The Insight: Your brain evolved to pay attention to things that might be important‚Äîbut sometimes it overdoes it, and you get hooked by something empty.

üéØ

Locus of Control: Where Do You Put Your Power?

Internal vs External: Do you believe your actions shape your life, or that luck/fate/others control what happens?

External Locus: You believe power comes from outside‚Äîother people's attention, owning things, how you look. You're constantly scanning for hooks because you need them as fuel.

Internal Locus: You believe power comes from inside‚Äîyour choices, your values, how you respond. Hooks lose their grip because you don't need them.

üîó

Connection to Trauma: Growing up in an unpredictable environment teaches you to surrender control‚Äîit kept you alive. But as an adult, this leaves you vulnerable to every shiny thing.

‚ö°

Triggers vs. Glimmers: What Your Nervous System Seeks

Your body scans for danger (triggers) and safety (glimmers). Both can "hook" you‚Äîbut one leads to harm, the other to healing.

üî¥ Triggers

Sensory cues that activate survival mode‚Äîfight, flight, or freeze. Based on past danger stored in your body.

üü¢ Glimmers

Tiny cues that tell your nervous system "you're safe." A warm color, a bird singing, a sunbeam. Activates your ventral vagus nerve (calm and connection).

üí°

The Practice: You can train yourself to notice glimmers. The more you notice moments of safety, the more your nervous system learns the world isn't all danger.

üîç

Gendlin's Focusing: Listen to Your Body's Wisdom

Your body knows things your brain hasn't figured out yet. Learn to tune into your "felt sense"‚Äîa bodily intuition not yet in words.

The Felt Sense: A heaviness in your chest, a knot in your stomach, a fluttering sensation. By gently paying attention and asking it questions, you unlock a "felt shift"‚Äîphysical release bringing new understanding.

üí°

When Hooked: Instead of chasing what caught your eye, turn inward: "What is this feeling in my body?" Stay with it gently, without trying to fix it. The shift is your body releasing something.

Somatic Exercises: Practical Tools for Reclaiming Agency

Specific practices to build inner power that doesn't depend on outside hooks.

ü§≤ The Container Exercise (Self-Holding)

How: Put one hand on your forehead, one on your heart. Feel the warmth. Imagine your body as a container that can hold any feeling without breaking.

Why: Creates physical boundaries. Tells your nervous system: "I can hold this. I'm still in control."

üì¢ The Voo Sound

How: Take a deep breath and make a low "voooo" sound like a foghorn. Feel it vibrate in your chest.

Why: The vibration stimulates your vagus nerve, calming your nervous system. Good for when you feel frozen.

üîÑ Pendulation

How: Gently move attention between distress and safety. Notice the tightness... then your feet on the floor... then back to tightness... then your breath.

Why: Teaches your nervous system it can move in and out of discomfort without being destroyed.

ü¶∂ Grounding Exercises

  • Heel drops: Lift heels, drop with a thud. Feel the impact. "It's safe to take up space."
  • Body scan: Slowly notice each body part from head to toe, just observing.
  • Box breathing: Inhale 4s, hold 4s, exhale 4s, hold 4s. Cues relaxation.

Emotional Transformation: From External to Inner Power

Understanding your emotional patterns and the journey from grasping power through possession to finding power within yourself.

ü•ó The Emotional Plate Metaphor (Your Salad)

You already have a smart way of thinking about your feelings. You know that when one feeling takes over (too "spicy"), you look for something to balance it‚Äîeven if you sometimes pick the wrong thing (something "greasy").

üí°

The Insight: You're naturally good at noticing your own emotional patterns. The Salad metaphor helps you see when one emotion dominates and what you reach for to compensate.

üîÑ Why Your Values Shift Depending on How You Feel

Your internal state acts like a filter that changes which values feel important. When you feel powerful, "owning" matters most. When you feel weak, you want safety. When you feel clear, you want real connection.

üí°

The Insight: You're not broken or inconsistent. Your values shift because your emotional state literally changes what your brain cares about at that moment. It's like wanting different foods depending on whether you're starving or just a little hungry.

üå∂Ô∏è The "Spicy and Greasy" Cycle (Compensatory Control)

Humans need to feel like life is orderly. When something shakes that feeling (losing power), it creates anxiety‚Äîthe "spicy" feeling. To cope, you reach for something that restores control. The "greasy" choice gives quick relief but causes problems later.

‚ö†Ô∏è

The Pattern: When life feels chaotic and you lose power, your brain panics and grabs for anything that makes you feel in control again‚Äîeven if it's unhealthy. Like fast food for your emotions: satisfying now, worse later.

üéØ Why You Want to "Own" (Objectification Theory)

When people feel powerful, they tend to see others as tools to achieve goals. The desire to "own" comes from a need to impose order on chaos. By reducing a complex person to a "target," you feel false mastery. But this backfires‚Äîit denies their humanity and damages your own sense of self.

üîó

The Truth: When you lose power, you look for something to control to feel better. But treating a person like an object doesn't fix the emptiness‚Äîit creates more problems and guilt afterward.

üë• The "Parts" of You (Internal Family Systems)

Your psyche has different "parts" with different jobs:

  • The Exile: Carries the pain of powerlessness and emptiness.
  • The Manager: Tries to get power through achievements to protect you from feeling the pain.
  • The Firefighter: When the Manager fails and pain breaks through, this part panics and grabs for quick fixes.
  • The Self: The "you" that watches all this and knows it's wrong. That calm part is your real self.
üí°

The Insight: Your mind is like a team. One part holds old wounds. Another tries to prevent pain by chasing success. When that fails, a third part freaks out and reaches for anything to make the pain stop. And there's a calm part watching it all‚Äîthat's your real self.

üå´Ô∏è Why You Feel Lost Right Now (The Neutral Zone)

You're in the "Neutral Zone"‚Äîa period between identities. You've let go of the old way (grabbing power through possession), but the new way (finding power within) isn't fully formed yet. This stage feels like confusion, emptiness, and not knowing what to do.

‚ú®

The Hope: This is normal and necessary. It's like being in a hallway between two rooms. You've left one room but haven't entered the next. The confusion means you're exactly where you need to be‚Äîgrowing.

üí™ What Inner Power Actually Is (Stoicism)

Real power comes from distinguishing what you can control from what you can't. You can't control whether people stay, whether projects succeed, or whether you lose status. But you can control your judgments, intentions, and how you respond to cravings.

üí°

The Truth: True power isn't about controlling others or holding onto things. Real power is being able to sit with discomfort, choose how you respond, and know your worth without needing proof from the outside. It's quiet. It doesn't need an audience. And no one can take it from you.

üéØ The Bottom Line: What This Means for You

You're in the middle of a profound transformation. You've moved from:

External Power
  • Grabbing
  • Owning
  • Controlling
‚Üí
Inner Power
  • Being
  • Connecting
  • Choosing

The confusion and emptiness aren't signs of failure. They're signs that the old patterns are dying and something new is being born. The urge to own was never really about the target‚Äîit was a misguided attempt to fill a void left by early wounds and lost power.

The fact that you're asking "what now?" and "what is inner power?" proves you're already on the right path. You're not reaching for the quick fix anymore. You're sitting in the silence, waiting to see what you actually hunger for.

üî•

The Power Was Always in You. You're just learning how to feel it now. Keep sitting with the questions. Keep noticing when the hook pulls. Keep choosing the pause instead of the grab.

üéØ What This All Means

When you get hooked by a color, a face, an appearance‚Äîit's not weakness. It's your brain doing what brains do.

The problem isn't the hook. The problem is when the hook becomes the only way you feel alive.

The solution is to build something inside that's stronger than any outside hook:

  • Notice when you're hooked
  • Feel what's happening in your body
  • Choose a different response‚Äîeven for a moment
  • Find safety and power inside yourself

The hook will always be there. But you don't have to be at its mercy anymore.
